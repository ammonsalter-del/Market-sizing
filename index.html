How Big Is Your Opportunity?

Whether you're pitching a startup to investors or proposing a new venture inside a large organisation — one question always comes first: how big is the market? This tool teaches you how to answer that with confidence and credibility.

🏁

Startup founders

🏢

Corporate innovators

Your mission

By the end of this exercise, you will have built a credible, evidence-backed market sizing for a new product or service — the kind that would hold up in a startup pitch, a corporate board presentation, an innovation committee review, or a business plan assessment.

You'll learn the industry-standard framework used by both startups and established companies (TAM-SAM-SOM), see it applied to real examples with real data, and then build your own — with every number traced back to a verifiable source.

What matters is not the precision of your numbers. It's the credibility of your evidence and the honesty of your assumptions.

Understanding Market Sizing

How to estimate the size of a market opportunity — and why the quality of your evidence matters more than the precision of your numbers

Why does market sizing matter?

Whether you're founding a startup or proposing a new venture inside an established organisation, you need to answer the same question: how big is the opportunity?

For startups

  • Investors demand it in every pitch deck
  • It shapes your fundraising story and valuation
  • It proves you understand your market deeply

For corporate ventures

  • Leadership won't approve resources without it
  • It justifies investment in new products or markets
  • It benchmarks opportunity against existing business

In both cases, it forces you to research your customers, understand competition, and think rigorously about your assumptions — which is where the real learning happens.

The #1 principle: Evidence and assumptions matter most Market sizing is NOT about getting a "correct" number — nobody can predict the future. What matters is: (1) where each number comes from — your evidence, and (2) what you are assuming — stated honestly. A rough estimate backed by credible sources beats a precise number pulled from thin air.

Before you start: WHAT and HOW?

Before touching any numbers, you need to answer two fundamental questions. Getting these wrong means the entire analysis is off target.

WHAT are you calculating?

Volume or value? Are you counting units sold (e.g., 500 devices) or revenue (e.g., £9M)?

What geographic scope? Greater London (9M people) is very different from Central London (1.5M). The UK is different from England alone.

What time period? Annual revenue? Lifetime value? Monthly users?

HOW will you calculate it?

What do you need to know? Think backwards from the answer. For a coffee shop in Central London:

  • Number of people in central London
  • How many would want takeaway coffee (right age, income)
  • How many cups they'd drink on average
  • Price per cup

Mapping this out first prevents you from collecting irrelevant data.

Write this down before you do anything else A clear statement like "I am estimating the annual revenue of a tomato delivery service in Greater London" keeps your entire analysis focused. Without it, you'll collect data for the wrong market.

Who is the customer? Value propositions by segment

The same product can serve different types of buyer — and each cares about different things. Understanding this matters for market sizing because different segments have different willingness to pay, different adoption rates, and different market sizes.

Example: A new hospital diagnostic device

💰
Budget holders

Care about: cost reduction, efficiency gains, ROI. They ask: "Will this save us money?"

🩹
Patients

Care about: less pain, fewer side effects, faster results. They ask: "Is this better for me?"

🩺
Clinical team

Care about: accuracy, speed, reliability. They ask: "Does this help me do my job better?"

Multi-sided marketplaces are even more complex

Some businesses serve multiple distinct customer groups simultaneously. Each side has a different market to size.

Deliveroo (three-sided)
Restaurants Delivery riders Consumers

Each group has different motivations and different market sizes to estimate.

LinkedIn (three-sided)
Individual users Recruiters Advertisers

The platform is free for users, but monetises through recruiters and advertisers.

For multi-sided markets: You may need to size each side separately. A food delivery market sizing needs: (1) how many restaurants would partner, (2) how many drivers are available, and (3) how many consumers would order. The smallest side often becomes the bottleneck.

Do they actually want your product?

Market sizing assumes there is demand for what you're selling. But having a potential market is not the same as having willing buyers. Before sizing the market, challenge your demand assumption.

The Warfarin test

Warfarin is an anticoagulant that treats blood clots and reduces the risk of heart attacks and strokes. The potential market is huge — millions of patients. But the most common side effect is bleeding, which requires blood tests at least every 12 weeks. This creates a major adoption barrier that a simple market size number wouldn't capture.

Ask yourself these questions before you start:

  • Does the customer know they have the problem? If not, your marketing costs will be much higher and adoption slower.
  • Are they currently solving it some other way? Switching costs and habits reduce your addressable market.
  • Are there hidden barriers to adoption? Regulations, side effects, infrastructure needs, cultural resistance.
  • Have you talked to any potential customers? Even 10 conversations can reveal whether your assumption of demand is realistic.
A market exists ≠ demand for your product exists Don't just count potential customers — think about what would stop them from buying. Build these barriers into your SAM and SOM reductions, and be honest about them.

TAM Total Addressable Market
Everyone who could possibly buy your product. The total market with no constraints.

SAM Serviceable Available Market
The segment you can actually reach — limited by geography, distribution, regulations, pricing.

SOM Serviceable Obtainable Market
What you can realistically capture, given competition, resources, and market conditions.

Think in ranges, not single numbers

No market sizing estimate is exact. You should always present your results as a range — an upper and lower scenario — rather than a single figure. This is called sensitivity analysis.

Finding and Evaluating Evidence

Your market sizing is only as good as the evidence behind it. This page helps you find reliable sources, avoid common traps, and verify what you find.

The golden rule: every number needs a source, and every source needs checking If you can't say where a number came from and why you trust it, it doesn't belong in your analysis. Assessors will always ask: "Where did you get that figure?"

Three tiers of evidence

Not all sources are equal. When you present your market sizing, assessors and investors will judge you on the quality of your evidence.

Strong

Official and verifiable

  • Government statistics (ONS, NHS Digital, Defra)
  • Regulatory filings (Companies House accounts)
  • Census data
  • Published academic research
  • Directly observable data (e.g., prices on a retailer website)

Moderate

Credible but check the method

  • Industry reports (Statista, IBISWorld, Mintel)
  • Trade body publications
  • News articles citing original data
  • Company press releases
  • Survey results (check sample size)

Weak

Avoid or clearly flag

  • Blog posts without sources
  • Wikipedia (use it to find sources, not as a source)
  • Social media claims
  • Unattributed "industry estimates"
  • Your own guess without reasoning

The limits of AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.)

Large Language Models can be incredibly useful for market sizing — but they have serious limitations you must understand.

AI tools can fabricate statistics that look completely real LLMs generate plausible-sounding text. They will confidently produce specific numbers, cite sources that don't exist, and format responses to look like research. This is called "hallucination" — the output reads like a fact but is invented.

What AI tools are good for:

  • Suggesting what to search for — "What data sources exist for the UK medical device market?"
  • Explaining frameworks — "How do I calculate TAM for a B2B product?"
  • Structuring your approach — "Help me think through the narrowing factors for my SAM"
  • Brainstorming assumptions — "What factors would reduce hospital adoption of a new device?"

What AI tools are NOT reliable for:

  • Specific market size numbers — "The UK medical device market is worth £X billion" (might be fabricated)
  • Specific URLs or source citations — LLMs often generate plausible but broken URLs
  • Current data — training data has a cutoff; prices and statistics change
  • Company-specific financials — revenue figures may be invented or outdated
The right workflow: use AI to guide your search, then verify every number yourself

Ask AI: "Where can I find NHS hospital statistics?" → It tells you: NHS Digital ERIC data → You go to digital.nhs.uk and download the actual dataset → You cite NHS Digital, not the AI tool.

How to verify a source

Before using any number, run through this checklist:

CheckAsk yourself
1. Can I find the original?Can I click through to the actual dataset, report, or page — not just someone quoting it?
2. Who published it?Is it a government body, academic institution, established research firm, or trade body? Or an anonymous blog?
3. When was it published?Is the data current enough? A 2018 figure for a fast-changing market may be misleading.
4. What's the methodology?For survey data: what was the sample size? For market estimates: how was it calculated?
5. Does it pass the smell test?Does this number seem reasonable given what else you know? If a source claims the UK tomato market is £50 billion, something is wrong.

Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to the most common questions about market sizing. Click any question to expand it.

Getting started
What exactly is market sizing?▶
Market sizing is the process of estimating how large a market opportunity is — typically expressed as a number of potential customers (volume) or potential revenue (value). It helps you understand whether an idea is worth pursuing, how to plan your business, and how to communicate the opportunity to investors or leadership.
Do I need to get the exact number right?▶
No — and nobody expects you to. Market sizing is about structured estimation, not precision. What matters is: (1) your logic makes sense, (2) your sources are credible and verifiable, and (3) your assumptions are clearly stated. An estimate that's within the right order of magnitude, backed by real data, is far more valuable than a "precise" number pulled from thin air.
Should I estimate volume (units) or value (£)?▶
It depends on your context. For a pitch or business plan, you usually want both: volume shows you understand how many customers are out there, and value shows the revenue opportunity. Start with whichever feels more natural — if you know the total number of potential customers, start with volume. If you know the total market spend from an industry report, start with value. You can always convert between them using your price.
How long should a market sizing exercise take?▶
For a first-pass estimate, a few hours of desk research can get you a credible ballpark. For a thorough analysis with verified sources, multiple approaches, and a sense check — expect to spend 1–2 days. The most time goes into finding and verifying data sources, not into the calculations themselves.
TAM, SAM, and SOM
What's the difference between TAM, SAM, and SOM?▶
TAM (Total Addressable Market) is everyone who could possibly buy your product — no constraints. SAM (Serviceable Available Market) is the portion you can realistically reach, given your geography, pricing, distribution, and other limits. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) is the share you can actually capture, given competition and your resources. Think of it as: TAM = the whole pie, SAM = the slices you could reach, SOM = what you'll actually eat.
Why do investors want to see TAM if it's unrealistic?▶
TAM shows the ceiling — the maximum scale of the opportunity if everything went perfectly. Investors care about TAM because it signals growth potential. A business targeting a £10M TAM has a very different trajectory than one targeting a £10B TAM. Even though you'll never capture all of it, a large TAM means there's room to grow. SAM and SOM then show you're realistic about your near-term opportunity.
How do I narrow from TAM to SAM?▶
Think about what limits your reach. Common narrowing factors include: geography (can you only sell in certain areas?), customer type (does your product only suit certain sizes or types of organisation?), regulations (are there licensing or compliance barriers?), distribution (can you actually get the product to them?), and pricing (does your price point exclude certain segments?). Each filter should ideally have a data source or a clearly stated assumption.
What's a realistic SOM for a new company?▶
For a brand-new entrant, capturing less than 1% of the total market is typical and credible. If you're claiming more than 5% of TAM, you need very strong justification — why would customers switch to you? A useful sense check: divide 100% by the number of competitors. If there are 20 players, the average share is 5%. A new entrant should be well below average unless they have a major advantage.
Top-down and bottom-up
What's the difference between top-down and bottom-up?▶
Top-down starts big and narrows: "The UK market is £X billion → our segment is Y% → we can capture Z%." Bottom-up starts small and builds: "We charge £X per unit → each customer buys Y times per year → we can reach Z customers." They're complementary — if both give a similar answer, your estimate is more credible. If they diverge a lot, it usually means you have a flawed assumption somewhere.
Do I need to do both approaches?▶
You should always try both if time allows. Top-down is often quicker and gives you a "big picture" view based on industry data. Bottom-up is more grounded in your specific product economics. Showing both — and discussing why they agree or differ — demonstrates thoroughness and critical thinking. If you can only do one, bottom-up is generally seen as more credible because it's built from your specific unit economics rather than general industry data.
Finding and using data
Where do I find market size data for free?▶
Start with government data: ONS (population, spending), NHS Digital (health), Defra (food/agriculture), Companies House (competitor revenue — free accounts downloads). Check if your university has Statista or IBISWorld access. Trade body websites often publish industry statistics. For pricing, retailer websites like Tesco.com give you directly observable, verifiable data. The Evidence tab in this tool lists the best free UK sources.
Can I use ChatGPT or AI tools for my market sizing?▶
Use AI tools to guide your search — ask "Where can I find UK hospital statistics?" and it might point you to NHS Digital. But never cite AI-generated numbers as your source. AI tools can fabricate statistics, invent URLs, and present hallucinated data with complete confidence. Always follow the chain: ask AI for direction → find the original source yourself → verify the data → cite the original source. See the Evidence tab for more detail on this.
What counts as a "good" source?▶
The best sources are verifiable, published, and from a credible institution. Government statistics (ONS, NHS Digital) are gold standard. Industry reports from established firms (Statista, IBISWorld) are good but check the methodology. Company accounts from Companies House are strong for competitor data. The weakest sources are blog posts without citations, social media, and unattributed "industry estimates." When in doubt, ask: "Could someone else find this exact number from this exact source?"
What if I can't find data for my specific market?▶
This is common, especially for new or niche markets. You have several options: (1) use a proxy market — find data for a similar, more established market and argue why it's relevant; (2) build up from observable data — count competitors, estimate their revenue from Companies House filings, and add up; (3) use survey data — even a small survey of 20–50 potential customers can give you adoption rate estimates. Always be transparent: "No direct market data exists, so we estimated by..." is much better than fabricating a number.
Common mistakes
What are the most common market sizing mistakes?▶
The biggest mistakes are: (1) Unsourced numbers — presenting figures with no evidence trail; (2) Confusing TAM with SOM — claiming you'll capture the whole market; (3) Using a single point estimate — not showing a range; (4) Not doing a sense check — your estimate might imply you'd be bigger than the market leader; (5) Citing AI-generated data — presenting ChatGPT's output as research; (6) Ignoring competition — acting as if no one else serves these customers.
My top-down and bottom-up numbers are very different — is that a problem?▶
A small difference (within 2–3x) is normal and expected — the two methods use different assumptions and data sources. A large difference (more than 5x) usually signals a flawed assumption in one approach. Common culprits: the top-down uses an overly broad market definition, the bottom-up assumes unrealistic customer reach, or one approach uses different pricing assumptions. Investigate the gap and explain it — this is actually a valuable part of the analysis.
How do I handle assumptions I can't verify?▶
State them clearly and explain your reasoning. For example: "We assume 1% of households would order tomato delivery. We cannot verify this directly, but we base it on grocery delivery adoption rates (Kantar data showing 12% of households use online grocery delivery, of which we assume a small fraction would specialise in a single product)." Also show what happens if the assumption is higher or lower — this is your sensitivity analysis. Honestly stated assumptions are respected; hidden ones destroy credibility.
Presenting your work
How should I present my market sizing in a pitch or business plan?▶
Lead with the headline numbers (TAM → SAM → SOM) and the revenue estimate. Then walk through the logic step by step — each narrowing factor, its source, and whether it's evidence or assumption. Show both top-down and bottom-up if possible, and discuss convergence. Include a sense check against a comparable company. End with a range (conservative/base/optimistic) and name your weakest assumptions. The narrative you build in the "Build Your Own" section of this tool follows this structure.
What questions will assessors or investors ask about my market sizing?▶
Expect questions like: "Where did that number come from?" (they'll probe your weakest data point), "What happens if that assumption is wrong?" (have your sensitivity analysis ready), "How does this compare to [competitor]?" (know your benchmarks), "Why would customers switch to you?" (demand validation), and "What's your realistic year-1 target?" (they want SOM, not TAM). The more you've verified your sources and thought through alternatives, the better you'll handle these.

Glossary

Key terms and definitions used throughout market sizing. Use the search box to find a specific term quickly.

Core framework
TAM (Total Addressable Market)
The total revenue opportunity available if you could sell to every possible customer with no constraints. Represents the theoretical ceiling of market demand for your product or service.
SAM (Serviceable Available Market)
The portion of TAM you can realistically reach, given constraints such as geography, pricing, distribution, customer type, and regulations. Your realistic addressable opportunity.
SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)
The share of SAM you can realistically capture, given competition and your resources. For new entrants, typically less than 1% of TAM. Your near-term revenue target.
Market sizing
The process of estimating how large a market opportunity is, typically expressed as a number of potential customers (volume) or potential revenue (value). Used to assess whether a business idea is worth pursuing.
Approaches
Top-down approach
Starting with a large, published market figure and narrowing it step by step using percentage filters (geography, segment, capture rate) to arrive at your estimate. Quick to do but can be optimistic.
Bottom-up approach
Starting with your unit economics — price per unit, units per customer, number of reachable customers — and building upward to a total figure. More grounded and credible for year-one estimates.
Convergence
When top-down and bottom-up estimates land in a similar range (within 2–3×). Convergence increases confidence in your estimate; large divergence signals a flawed assumption somewhere.
Sensitivity analysis
Varying your key assumptions to show a range of outcomes: conservative (pessimistic inputs), base case (best estimates), and optimistic (favourable inputs). Demonstrates maturity and acknowledges uncertainty.
Data & evidence
Evidence
A data point backed by a verifiable external source that someone else could independently check. Examples: government statistics, company filings, published industry reports.
Assumption
A reasoned estimate made where hard data is not available. Must be clearly labelled, logically justified, and ideally tested via sensitivity analysis. Honest assumptions are respected; hidden ones destroy credibility.
Proxy market
A similar, more established market used as a reference when direct data for your specific market doesn't exist. Must be accompanied by clear reasoning for why the proxy is relevant.
Sense check
Comparing your estimate against a known real-world benchmark (such as a competitor's revenue or a published market figure) to verify your numbers are in a reasonable range.
Market types & customers
B2B (Business-to-Business)
A market where your customers are organisations, not individuals. Market sizing counts organisations, departments, or decision-makers. Example: selling medical devices to hospitals.
B2C (Business-to-Consumer)
A market where your customers are individual people or households. Market sizing counts people, households, or usage occasions. Example: selling tomato delivery to London households.
Multi-sided marketplace
A platform that serves two or more distinct customer groups who depend on each other. Each side has its own TAM. Example: Deliveroo connects restaurants, riders, and consumers (three-sided).
Customer segmentation
Dividing your potential market into distinct groups with different needs, behaviours, or value propositions. Important because different segments may require different market sizing approaches.
Valuation & pricing
Volume (market size)
Market size expressed as a number of potential customers or units sold. Shows you understand how many buyers exist. Often the starting point for bottom-up sizing.
Value (market size)
Market size expressed in monetary terms (revenue). Calculated as volume × price. Shows the revenue opportunity. Often the starting point for top-down sizing using industry reports.
Unit economics
The revenue and costs associated with a single unit of your product or service. In market sizing, this means your price per unit, purchase frequency, and customer lifetime — the building blocks of bottom-up estimation.
Demand validation
Testing whether customers actually want your specific product, not just that a market exists. A market existing does not prove demand for your solution. Methods include surveys, prototypes, and observational tests.
Data sources
ONS (Office for National Statistics)
The UK's national statistical authority. Publishes data on population, demographics, spending, employment, and economic indicators. Gold-standard source for UK market sizing.
NHS Digital
The official source for health and social care data in England. Publishes hospital counts, bed numbers, patient statistics, and workforce data. Essential for health-related B2B market sizing.
Companies House
UK government register of companies. Provides free access to company filings including annual accounts — useful for estimating competitor revenues as part of a bottom-up or sense-check approach.
Statista / IBISWorld
Commercial market research platforms that publish industry size estimates, growth rates, and market breakdowns. Often available through university library subscriptions. Useful for top-down TAM figures.
